## Target PIs (top 3)

### Jack Harris — Professor of Physics and Applied Physics; Yale Quantum Institute
- **Group:** Harris Lab (quantum optomechanics, non-Hermitian physics, superfluid helium optomechanics)
- **Group size:** ~6–8 students; established group
- **Recent achievements and papers:**
  - *Complex Berry phase and steady-state geometric amplification in non-Hermitian systems* — Nature Physics, 2025 — demonstrates geometric phases and amplification in quantum open systems; foundational quantum mechanics of loss and gain in optical resonators
  - *Optomechanical control of long-lived bulk acoustic phonons in the quantum regime* — Nature Physics, 2025 — quantum-coherent control of long-lived acoustic modes; foundation for quantum transduction and quantum memory
  - *Resolving the topology of encircling multiple exceptional points* — Nature Communications, 2024 — topology of non-Hermitian parameter spaces; foundational quantum optics
  - Breakthrough Prize 2023 co-recipient (with Regal, Kippenberg)
- **Alumni placements:** Outstanding — Harris group alumni at MIT, Caltech, top quantum optomechanics groups, quantum startups
- **Frank's specific hook:** Harris's quantum optomechanics and non-Hermitian physics is foundational quantum optics — closer to Frank's academic identity (experimental physics at the quantum-classical boundary) than to his specific quantum networking career goal. However, Harris's acoustic phonon work (quantum regime, long coherence) is directly relevant to quantum transduction and quantum memory — both quantum networking primitives. Frank's experimental optics skill set (phase-profile design, intensity measurement, coherent optical control) transfers directly to the optical drive and readout systems in Harris's optomechanical experiments. The non-Hermitian physics angle also resonates with Frank's structured light work (which involves complex amplitude and phase profiles). Harris is arguably the most intellectually stimulating PI choice at Yale.

### Peter Rakich — Donna L. Dubinsky Professor of Applied Physics; Yale Quantum Institute
- **Group:** Rakich Lab (nonlinear photonics, Brillouin optomechanics, photonic integrated circuits for communications)
- **Group size:** ~6–10 students; high-output group
- **Recent achievements and papers:**
  - *Photonic chip-based low-noise microwave oscillator* — Nature, 2024 — photonic chip that generates ultra-stable microwave signals via stimulated Brillouin scattering; directly applicable to optical communication timing and synchronization
  - *A terahertz-bandwidth non-magnetic isolator* — Nature Photonics, 2025 — non-reciprocal photonic device enabling one-way light propagation without magnets; a key component for quantum photonic network isolation
  - *Electrically interfaced Brillouin-active waveguide for microwave photonic measurements* — Nature Communications, 2024 — Brillouin-active waveguides for microwave photonic signal processing
  - *Optomechanical resource for fault-tolerant quantum computing* — arXiv, May 2025 — Rakich group now explicitly targeting quantum information applications of optomechanical systems
  - *Low-noise microwave generation with an air-gap optical reference cavity* — APL Photonics, 2024
- **Alumni placements:** Strong — Rakich alumni at top academic and industry positions in quantum photonics and optical communications; his group bridges fundamental quantum optics and applied photonics engineering
- **Frank's specific hook:** Rakich's nonlinear photonic chip work is the most direct connection to Frank's optical networking career goal at Yale. Nonlinear photonics on chip (Brillouin scattering, frequency conversion, optical signal processing) is the physical layer of quantum communication networks. Rakich's 2025 quantum computing preprint signals his group is explicitly pivoting toward quantum information applications of photonic systems — exactly Frank's target space. Frank's structured light and phase-profile expertise is applicable to the coherent optical driving of Rakich's Brillouin-active waveguide systems.

### Charles D. Brown II — Assistant Professor of Physics; Yale Quantum Institute
- **Group:** Brown Lab (ultracold atoms, optical lattices, quantum simulation, AMO physics)
- **Group size:** ~2–4 students (joined Yale recently; actively recruiting)
- **Recent recognition:** 2024 Air Force Office of Scientific Research (AFOSR) Young Investigator Program award; 2023 Joseph A. Johnson Award for Excellence
- **Research focus:** Quantum simulation of ordered phases and dynamics using ultracold atoms in optical lattices; single-/few-/many-body quantum physics; quantum gas microscope techniques; interface of AMO and condensed matter physics
- **Frank's specific hook:** Brown's ultracold atom experiments in optical lattices use precisely structured optical potentials (including SLM-generated lattice geometries) for single-site-resolved imaging and control. Frank's optical design and SLM skills are directly applicable. Brown is a young faculty member (good for mentorship and collaboration), recently funded, and likely actively recruiting — making cold email outreach particularly valuable before application season.

## Funding details
- **Years of guaranteed funding:** 5+ years (minimum guaranteed for all Yale Physics PhD students)
- **Annual stipend:** $50,777/year (12-month appointment, 2025–26, confirmed); full tuition ($50,900/year) + paid health coverage for all PhD students; family support subsidy available
- **Funding source:** Fellowship + TA/RA mix by year; advisor RA from Year 2+
- **Summer support:** Included in 12-month appointment
- **International-student eligibility:** Confirmed — all Yale PhD students funded regardless of citizenship; NSF GRFP ineligible (non-citizen); Yale internal YQI fellowships may be available
- **COL note:** New Haven COL is moderate — meaningfully lower than Cambridge, NYC, or Bay Area; $50,777 in New Haven has significantly more purchasing power than $51,228 in Cambridge; best stipend-to-COL ratio among all reach programs

## Program quirks
- **Yale Quantum Institute (YQI):** Cross-departmental institute spanning Physics, Applied Physics, EE, CS — Brown, Harris, Rakich, Devoret (superconducting circuits), Regal-adjacent faculty all affiliated; quantum ecosystem is broad and collaborative
- **Cohort size:** Yale Physics admits ~25–35 students/year; more intimate than MIT but larger than Caltech
- **Qualifier:** Not confirmed for current format — Yale Physics has historically had a written qualifying exam plus an oral component; verify current policy
- **New Haven location:** 2 hours from NYC by Amtrak; 3 hours from Syracuse by car; Northeast corridor is easily accessible without being in the most expensive metro; good balance of Northeast access and affordability
- **Harris Breakthrough Prize lineage:** Harris's 2023 Breakthrough Prize (with Regal and Kippenberg) for optomechanics positions his group at the absolute frontier of the field; being a Harris group PhD is a strong career signal
