## Target PIs (top 3)

### Jeff Thompson — Associate Professor, ECE; PQI Member
- **Group:** Thompson Group (quantum networking, atom–nanophotonic interfaces, quantum transduction)
- **Group size:** ~5–8 students; actively publishing
- **Department:** ECE (not Physics) — see routing note below
- **Recent achievements and papers:**
  - *Single Er³⁺ ions in CaWO₄ coupled to nanophotonic cavities for indistinguishable single-photon emission and spin-photon entanglement in telecom band* — 2024/2025 — Er³⁺ at telecom wavelength coupled to nanophotonic structures; directly enables quantum networking at standard fiber wavelengths
  - *Quantum transduction: converting single microwave photons to single optical sideband photons* — ongoing experimental work — microwave-to-optical transduction, a key quantum networking primitive
  - Earlier: *Integrated optical multi-ion quantum logic* (Nature 2016, from MIT PhD with Chuang) — foundational integrated photonic quantum computing work
  - Publication in Optica 11, 227-233 (2024)
- **Alumni placements:** Group growing (joined Princeton ~2018); some PhD graduates placed at top academic postdocs and quantum industry
- **Frank's specific hook:** Thompson's atom–nanophotonic coupling work is the most direct quantum networking experimental program at Princeton. His telecom-band spin-photon entanglement using Er³⁺ ions in engineered nanophotonic cavities is precisely the hardware Frank's quantum networking career goal points toward. Frank's SLM-based optical field engineering maps onto the optical addressing and characterization side of Thompson's nanophotonic interface experiments. **However, Thompson is in ECE — Frank must apply to Princeton ECE PhD to work with him directly.**

### Nathalie de Leon — Associate Professor, ECE; Co-Director PQI
- **Group:** de Leon Group (diamond NV centers, quantum sensors, quantum networks)
- **Group size:** ~6–8 students; established group
- **Department:** ECE (not Physics)
- **Recent papers:**
  - Nitrogen color centers in diamonds (Physical Review X, 2025)

Frank's experimental optics background (optical addressing, coherent control via light) is applicable to the optical pumping and readout of NV center spin states. **Also in ECE — same routing issue as Thompson.**

### Waseem Bakr — Associate Professor of Physics; PQI Member
- **Group:** Bakr Lab (ultracold atoms, quantum gas microscopy, quantum simulation)
- **Group size:** ~5–7 students
- **Department:** Physics — accessible directly via Physics PhD
- **Research focus:** Quantum simulation of strongly correlated systems using ultracold atoms in optical lattices; quantum gas microscope techniques (single-atom resolved imaging); Bose-Hubbard and Fermi-Hubbard model physics
- **Frank's specific hook:** Bakr's quantum gas microscope experiments use sophisticated optical imaging and manipulation systems — structured optical beams and SLMs are central tools. Frank's SLM phase-profile expertise is directly transferable to quantum gas microscope optical design. While Bakr's primary focus is quantum simulation (not quantum networking), his AMO quantum optics platform is closely related to Frank's experimental identity. Best as a Physics-dept fallback if Thompson is unreachable via ECE routing.

## Department routing decision: Physics vs. ECE

**The two best-fit PIs (Thompson and de Leon) are both in Princeton ECE, not Physics.**

- **Physics PhD route:** Gives direct access to Bakr and other Physics-dept quantum optics faculty; accessing Thompson or de Leon requires explicit cross-department co-advising arrangement — possible but not guaranteed
- **ECE PhD route:** Gives direct access to Thompson and de Leon; requires ECE-oriented SoP emphasis (photonic devices, quantum information hardware engineering) rather than pure physics framing
- **Recommendation:** Frank should decide whether to apply to **Princeton ECE** (targeting Thompson/de Leon) or **Princeton Physics** (targeting Bakr + cross-advising with Thompson). Applying to both is an option but adds overhead and fee. Cold email Thompson or de Leon to ask which PhD program they prefer to admit students through — this is the single most important piece of pre-application intelligence for Princeton.

## Funding details
- **Years of guaranteed funding:** All Princeton PhD students receive full multi-year funding (Physics and ECE both)
- **Annual stipend:** Not publicly listed for Physics or ECE specifically; Princeton Molecular Biology confirmed at $54,216 for 2025–26; Physics likely comparable; estimate $42,000–54,000 range
- **Funding source:** First year: 10-month First-Year Fellowship (tuition + stipend + fees); Year 2+: RA or TA; advisor-funded RA typically from Year 2
- **Summer support:** Standard inclusion in RA appointments
- **International-student eligibility:** Fully eligible; NSF GRFP ineligible (non-citizen); Princeton internal fellowships may be available
- **COL note:** Princeton NJ is moderate-to-affordable relative to NYC; housing is expensive but manageable on an Ivy stipend

## Program quirks
- **Princeton Quantum Initiative (PQI):** Cross-departmental hub (Physics, ECE, Chemistry, CS) — students in any department can attend PQI seminars, workshops, and industry events; mitigates the departmental silo problem somewhat
- **Cohort size:** Physics ~30–40 students/year; ECE similar; both competitive
- **IBM Princeton proximity:** IBM Research Princeton is a 20-minute drive — quantum computing industry access is unusual for academic programs; students in Thompson/de Leon groups frequently collaborate
- **Qualifier (Physics):** Primarily research-focused, not just written exam — verify current format with Physics graduate coordinator
- **Bell Labs heritage:** Princeton NJ sits within the historical Bell Labs corridor (now Nokia Bell Labs in Murray Hill, NJ) — strong quantum photonics research lineage in the region
