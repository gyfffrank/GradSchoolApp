## Target PIs (top 3)

### Vladan Vuletić — Cecil and Ida Green Professor of Physics; MIT-Harvard Center for Ultracold Atoms
- **Group:** Vuletić Lab (neutral atoms, cavity QED, quantum information, quantum networking)
- **Group size:** ~8–12 students; large, productive group
- **Recent achievements and papers:**
  - *Error-Detected Quantum Operations with Neutral Atoms Mediated by an Optical Cavity* — Science, March 2025 — fault-tolerant quantum operations using optical cavity error detection; directly relevant to quantum networking hardware
  - *Fault-tolerant optical interconnects for neutral-atom arrays* — Physical Review Research, March 2025 — optical interconnects for scaling neutral-atom quantum processors; maps directly to quantum internet architecture
  - *Towards telecommunication-band quantum networking for atom arrays* — Optica Quantum 2.0 Conference, 2025 — explicit quantum networking at telecom wavelengths using atom arrays
  - *Control and Entanglement of Individual Rydberg Atoms Near a Nanoscale Device* — PRL, March 2024 — atom-nanophotonic interface; directly analogous to Thompson's work at Princeton
- **Alumni placements:** Outstanding — Vuletić group PhDs placed at MIT CSAIL, Harvard, Caltech, national labs, Google, IBM; Jun Ye (JILA) trained with similar lineage
- **Frank's specific hook:** Vuletić's 2025 work on fault-tolerant optical interconnects and telecom-band quantum networking is the physical implementation of Frank's quantum internet career goal using neutral atoms. Frank's SLM-based phase-profile design and structured light experiments (Schwarzschild lenses, pendular eigenstates) map directly onto the spatial light manipulation required for optical addressing of neutral atom arrays. The cavity QED framework also connects to Frank's structured optical beam work through the Helmholtz–Schrödinger equivalence he demonstrated experimentally.

### Dirk Englund — Associate Professor, EECS; MIT Research Laboratory of Electronics (RLE)
- **Group:** Quantum Photonics Laboratory (quantum photonic integrated circuits, silicon color centers, photonic computing)
- **Group size:** ~10–15 students; large, high-output group; EECS, not Physics — but accessible to Physics PhD students via RLE
- **Recent achievements and papers:**
  - *Photonic deep learning chip* — June 2025 — large-scale programmable photonic processor
  - *Spatial-spectral hypermultiplexed photonic processor* — January 2026 — photonic information processing at unprecedented scale
  - *Light-emitting structures for quantum computing* — December 2024 — scalable quantum light sources integrated with photonic circuits
- **Alumni placements:** Very strong — Englund alumni at top academic institutions, MIT, IBM Research, Google, quantum startups
- **Frank's specific hook:** Englund's quantum photonic integrated circuits work directly overlaps with Frank's optical networking career goal. His group designs and characterizes photonic structures for quantum information processing — Frank's SLM expertise in designing phase profiles for structured beams is directly transferable to the programmable photonic circuits Englund's group builds. **Note:** Englund is in EECS, not Physics — Physics PhD students can co-advise with EECS faculty but this requires explicit arrangement; confirm Englund accepts Physics PhD students before applying.

### Paola Cappellaro — Ford Professor of Engineering, NSE and Physics (joint)
- **Group:** Quantum Engineering Group (quantum sensing, quantum networking, quantum simulation with NV centers and other solid-state spins)
- **Group size:** ~6–8 students
- **Research:** Coherent control of solid-state quantum systems; quantum networks via spin-photon interfaces; quantum sensing for fundamental physics and biomedical imaging
- **Frank's specific hook:** Cappellaro's quantum networking work via spin-photon interfaces connects to Frank's optical networking career goal; her group uses coherent optical control of solid-state spins — directly accessible from Frank's structured light background. Less central to Frank's experimental optics identity than Vuletić, but provides a fallback option in the same quantum networking space.

## Funding details
- **Years of guaranteed funding:** Full duration of PhD (implicit in MIT's 100% funding model for all admitted students)
- **Annual stipend:** $51,228/year (12-month; confirmed June 2025 rate; includes 3.25% increase from prior year); note: AY rate is $4,269/month
- **Funding source:** RA/TA mix; MIT RAs are well-funded by advisor grants; Year 1 typically TA
- **Summer support:** Included in 12-month RA appointment
- **International-student eligibility:** Fully eligible for all RA/TA funding at MIT; NSF GRFP ineligible (non-citizen); MIT internal fellowships (Presidential, other) may be available
- **COL note:** Cambridge is one of the most expensive US cities; $51k is survivable but not comfortable — verify housing options (on-campus grad housing exists at MIT)

## Program quirks
- **Competition is extreme:** MIT Physics is routinely cited as the single hardest physics PhD admit in the US — ~45 students from 2,000+ applicants (2.25% admit rate). Frank should apply with full effort and zero expectation.
- **Cohort culture:** Small enough to know everyone, large enough to have depth; MIT physics culture is intensely research-focused; less social-support infrastructure than smaller programs
- **Vuletić and Englund both run large, busy groups:** Students can get excellent training but advisor face time requires proactive communication; not the best environment for students who need high mentorship contact
- **Cross-departmental access:** RLE gives MIT Physics students access to EECS PIs (Englund, Soljačić, others) — this is MIT's structural advantage; the quantum photonics ecosystem spans departments
