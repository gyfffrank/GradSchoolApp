## Target PIs (top 3)

### David Snoke — Professor of Physics
- **Group:** Snoke Group (polariton condensates, exciton-polariton physics, microcavity quantum optics)
- **Group size:** ~4–6 students; established group
- **Research focus:** Exciton-polariton condensates (Bose-Einstein condensation of light-matter quasi-particles); polariton quantum fluid dynamics; nonlinear optical phenomena in semiconductor microcavities; fundamental quantum optics of strongly coupled light-matter systems
- **Frank's specific hook:** Snoke's polariton condensate work is quantum optics at its most interesting — photons coupled to excitons form quasi-particles that can Bose-condense, exhibiting quantum coherence at elevated temperatures. The optical addressing and phase-coherent measurement of polariton condensates involves precisely structured optical fields — Frank's SLM background is applicable. Snoke's nonlinear optical physics also connects to Frank's nonlinear dynamics background (Koopman, Chua's circuit). While this is more fundamental quantum optics than quantum networking, it is genuine quantum optical science.

### Nathan Youngblood — Associate Professor, ECE; Adjunct in Physics & Astronomy (Spring 2025+)
- **Group:** Youngblood Photonics Lab (photonic memory devices, neuromorphic photonics, integrated photonic circuits)
- **Group size:** ~3–5 students (growing)
- **Recent recognition:** NSF CAREER Award (2024)
- **Research focus:** Phase-change materials for photonic memory and computing; reconfigurable photonic integrated circuits; neuromorphic (brain-inspired) photonic computing; optical analog computing
- **Frank's specific hook:** Youngblood's reconfigurable photonic integrated circuits use programmable optical elements (conceptually analogous to SLM phase modulation) for photonic computing applications. His adjunct Physics appointment makes him accessible. However, Youngblood's work is more photonic computing than quantum networking — the quantum relevance is indirect.

### Gurudev Dutt — Associate Professor of Physics
- **Group:** Dutt Lab (quantum control, NV centers in diamond, quantum sensing)
- **Group size:** ~3–5 students
- **Research:** Coherent quantum control of NV centers and other solid-state spin systems; quantum sensing; quantum information in condensed matter hosts; optically addressable spin qubits
- **Frank's specific hook:** Dutt's NV center quantum control work uses structured optical pulses and coherent optical addressing — relevant to Frank's structured light background. NV centers are a platform for quantum networking nodes (spin-photon interfaces for entanglement distribution), connecting to Frank's career goal. Less direct than Figueroa (Stony Brook) or Waks (Maryland) for quantum networking, but a solid quantum optics experimental group.

## Program quirks
- **Pittsburgh Quantum Institute (PQI):** Multi-institutional with CMU (strong in quantum networking theory — Kevin Chen, Mohamed Hafez) and Duquesne; students at Pitt Physics in quantum-adjacent groups can attend PQI events, seminars, and workshops; this cross-institutional access partially compensates for Pitt's individual program limitations
- **January deadline:** Pitt's later deadline is a practical advantage — Frank can complete this application after all December deadlines are submitted; treat it as a fallback that requires minimal additional work
- **CMU proximity:** Carnegie Mellon is a 15-minute drive; CMU has strong quantum networking and computing research; PQI fellowship or collaboration could give Frank access to CMU research even while enrolled at Pitt
- **Pittsburgh affordability:** Pittsburgh is consistently rated one of the most affordable major US cities for graduate students; even an unconfirmed stipend would likely support a comfortable lifestyle here
